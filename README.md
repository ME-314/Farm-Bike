# Farm-Bike

## Preamble

The **Farm-Bike** is a simultaneous engineering project carried out at EPFL within the *Durabilité* unit. The objective of the project is to create a modular human-powered source of mechanical power for agricultural machines. The project covers the complete development of the prototype, from specification and design to manufacturing, assembly, testing and future improvements.

The machine consists of an aluminium extrusion frame onto which a bicycle drivetrain is mounted. A custom bottom bracket mount, hub, pillow block and output pulley system were designed and manufactured to transmit the power generated by the rider to external farm machinery through a belt transmission.

The CAD models for this project can be found in the `CadParts` folder. The manufacturing and assembly drawings are located in the `Drafts` folder, and the calculation spreadsheet is located in the `Documentation` folder.

## Specification

### Functionality

- Convert human power into useful shaft work.
- Provide an easily adjustable transmission ratio.
- Allow quick coupling to different agricultural machines.

Several quantitative assumptions were used to dimension the prototype:

- Rider cadence: approximately **70 rpm**.
- Continuous mechanical input power: approximately **250 W**.
- Target machine speed range: approximately **50–150 rpm**.
- Adjustable saddle height, handlebar position and frame geometry to accommodate different users.

### Work Environment

- Resist an outdoor working environment in a central European climate.
- Resist storage in humid or wet conditions.

### Durability

- Use standard parts wherever possible to ensure long-term availability.
- Use conventional machining processes for custom parts to improve repairability.
- Use robust components to extend service life.
- Keep maintenance procedures simple.

## Design

The design of the Farm-Bike was driven by the specification above and by the objective of making the prototype replicable, repairable and adaptable. Quality parts and durable materials were preferred over the lowest-cost alternatives.

### Frame

The frame is made from Item modular aluminium extrusions, specifically profile 8 series. The chosen 40 mm × 40 mm T-slot profile provides a good compromise between stiffness, availability, ease of assembly and modularity. Equivalent 40 mm × 40 mm aluminium profiles can be used if the exact Item profile is not available.

The modular frame was especially useful during testing, since the geometry of the prototype could be adapted on site without welding or manufacturing a new frame.

### Drivetrain

The drivetrain is based on standard bicycle components. This solution was chosen because bicycle drivetrains are robust, relatively low-cost, easy to maintain and widely available.

The following components were used without major modification from their retail configuration:

- Cassette.
- Derailleur and shifter.
- Handlebars and stem.
- Crankset and bottom bracket.
- Seat post and saddle.

The Farm-Bike is designed to power agricultural implements that are often hand-cranked and operate at low rotational speeds. With the chosen cassette, chainring and pulley diameters, and for an assumed pedalling cadence of 70 rpm, the output speed can vary between approximately **47 rpm** and **137 rpm**. If another machine requires a different speed range, the driven pulley diameter can be changed accordingly.

The final connection between the Farm-Bike and the agricultural machine is made using a belt transmission. This provides a simple and flexible coupling, tolerates small alignment errors and can slip under excessive load before damaging more critical drivetrain components.

The assumed rider input is a cadence of **70 rpm** and a mechanical power of **250 W**. The crank torque is calculated as:

```math
T_{\mathrm{crank}} = \frac{P \cdot 60}{n \cdot 2\pi} = 34.10~\mathrm{N\,m}
```

The high-speed case uses the smallest cassette sprocket, while the low-speed case uses the largest cassette sprocket. The final pulley stage uses a **71 mm drive pulley** and a **112 mm driven pulley**.

### Machine-Specific Parts

#### Bottom Bracket Shell

The bottom bracket shell attaches a standard BSA bicycle bottom bracket to the frame. It is a weldment made from a donor bicycle bottom bracket shell and a 40 mm × 30 mm rectangular steel tube. This approach avoids machining a full threaded shell from billet and reduces material use.

#### Hub

The hub is the central transmission component of the Farm-Bike. It mounts the cassette and the pulley, transmitting the power generated by the rider to the belt drive. A flywheel was also mounted on the axle to smooth the power delivery, although testing showed that the larger inertial wheels already present on some agricultural machines were more effective.

#### Pillow Block

The pillow block houses the main bearings and attaches the hub assembly to the frame. It maintains the alignment between the two bearings and ensures smooth operation. The prototype pillow block was machined from 6061 aluminium and split into two pinned pieces due to available stock limitations.

The design uses two **6005-2Z** deep-groove ball bearings. This bearing reference corresponds to a 25 mm inner diameter, 47 mm outer diameter and 12 mm width. The `2Z` suffix indicates metal shields on both sides, which help protect the rolling elements from dust while keeping friction low.

#### Axle

The axle includes 25 mm bearing seats and a keyway to transmit torque to the flywheel and pulley. A donor freehub body was chemically bonded to the axle to mount the cassette while avoiding complex machining operations.

#### Derailleur Hanger

A derailleur hanger from a donor bicycle frame was used to reproduce the mounting geometry of a conventional bicycle. A custom 3 mm aluminium sheet mount positions the hanger correctly relative to the axle and cassette.

## Manufacturing

This section summarizes the main manufacturing operations. It assumes that the reader is familiar with basic machining and fabrication methods.

### Bottom Bracket Shell

The donor bottom bracket shell was cut from a steel frame and turned down to a constant diameter. A close-fitting aluminium plug was inserted inside the threads during turning to provide tailstock support. The hole in the rectangular tube was roughed with a hole saw and fitted by hand with a file. The parts were then TIG welded and the BSA thread was chased.

TIG welding was used for the prototype because it provides good control on thin steel sections. However, the design does not strictly require TIG welding. A future version could replace the welded bottom bracket shell with a clamped or bolted support to improve field replicability.

### Axle

The axle was machined using standard turning operations. Loctite 603 retaining compound was used to bond the freehub body to the axle, avoiding the need to press the hardened freehub body onto the axle.

### Pillow Block

The pillow block was machined from two separate pieces of aluminium stock. The operations were carried out in the following order:

- Squaring the stock to size.
- Drilling and reaming the 4 mm pin bores.
- Centre drilling the main bore.
- Pressing the two parts together.
- Milling the locating slot.
- Drilling the M8 clearance holes.
- Boring half of the through bore in a lathe using the centre drill hole as reference.
- Boring the bearing seat.
- Flipping the part and repeating the boring operations.
- Deburring and chamfering.

### Flywheel

The flywheel was cut from 5 mm S235 structural steel and assembled from pinned plates. Fibre laser cutting was used for the prototype because it was available and efficient. However, the geometry is simple enough to be produced with more accessible methods such as drilling, sawing, filing and balancing.

## Procurement Guide

Normalized industrial parts for this project can be bought from well-established suppliers. The prototype parts were sourced from Norelem, but equivalent parts can be sourced from:

- Misumi.
- RS Components.
- McMaster-Carr.
- JLCMC.
- Item24, or another supplier of 40 mm × 40 mm aluminium profiles.

To reduce the ecological impact of the prototype, second-hand or recovered components were used where this did not affect safety, lifetime or operation. Aluminium profiles and relevant hardware were recovered from decommissioned machinery. Bicycle components were second-hand and sourced from *Point Vélo* on the EPFL campus.

## User Guide

### Use Instructions

Before operating the machine, place it on a stable and level surface. The frame should not rock or move under load. All fasteners securing the aluminium profiles, pillow block, bottom bracket shell, seat post and handlebar assembly should be checked for tightness. The pulley, flywheel and cassette should be visually inspected to ensure that they are correctly seated and that no foreign object is interfering with the drivetrain.

The user should adjust the saddle height and handlebar position to obtain a comfortable pedalling position. The saddle height should allow the leg to remain slightly bent at the bottom of the pedal stroke. The rear derailleur can be shifted using the standard handlebar shifter. The front chainring selection must be changed manually only when the machine is fully stationary.

To start the machine, the user should begin pedalling progressively and avoid sudden impacts on the pedals. Once the system is rotating steadily, the selected gear ratio can be adjusted depending on the required output speed and the resistance of the coupled machine.

Before coupling the Farm-Bike to an external machine, the user must ensure that the driven machine is compatible with the output speed, torque and rotation direction of the system. The coupling should be securely mounted and aligned with the pulley or output shaft to avoid vibrations, belt slipping or premature wear. During operation, hands, clothing and loose objects must be kept away from the chain, cassette, pulley, flywheel and any rotating element.

To stop the machine, the user should reduce pedalling effort progressively and allow the drivetrain and flywheel to slow down naturally. The user should not attempt to stop the flywheel, pulley or chain by hand.

### Maintenance Guide

Maintenance intervals are guidelines only and should be adjusted according to usage and storage conditions.

- Before each use: inspect fasteners and drivetrain.
- Every 7–10 hours: clean and lubricate the chain.
- Every 30–50 hours: degrease and lubricate the chain.
- Every 100 hours: inspect the drivetrain for wear.
- Every 150 hours: disassemble and inspect the hub and crankset.

Parts deemed inoperable during inspection should be replaced. For more detailed information on fitting, adjusting and maintaining bicycle-specific components, refer to the Shimano General Operations Manual `DM-GN0001`.

### Troubleshooting Guide

| Issue | Likely cause | Corrective action |
|---|---|---|
| Unusual noise | Loose fastener | Verify and tighten fasteners. |
| Chain skips under power | Worn drivetrain components | Replace the chain. If the issue persists, replace the cassette and chainrings. |
| Chain jumps between gears | Shifting not adjusted | Adjust the shift cable. |
| Chain falls off the top or bottom of the cassette | Derailleur limits out of adjustment | Adjust the derailleur limits. |
| Excessive friction in drivetrain under no load | Debris stuck or tangled in the drivetrain | Inspect the drivetrain and remove debris. |

## Testing

### Initial Testing: 14/05/2026 (Week 12)

The first functional test was carried out directly at the Bassenges farm. The Farm-Bike prototype was connected to two existing machines: the polenta grinder and the corn sheller. Pulleys were installed on both machines, after which the bike was coupled to the system and tested. The first test confirmed that the Farm-Bike could drive the machines through the belt transmission.

During operation, Timothée identified an important ergonomic issue: in the initial configuration, the bike was not facing the machines. This meant that a single operator could not comfortably pedal and load the machines at the same time. The modular aluminium profile frame made it possible to rearrange the prototype on site without manufacturing new parts. The pulley attachment was repositioned to face the opposite direction and was moved to the front of the bike.

### Second Testing Session and Adjustments: Week 13

The second testing session allowed the team to further adapt the Farm-Bike prototype based on the first trial. The chain length was adjusted and the seating position was modified. The overall design proved functional, but the shorter chain affected shifting performance: gear changes became less smooth and the most extreme gear-ratio settings became more difficult to use.

Timothée also suggested keeping the inertial wheel attached to the corn sheller. In response, the pulley transmission was modified. Instead of removing the inertial wheel, the pulley system was attached to the rear side of the transmission axle, replacing an unused gear. This allowed the inertial wheel to be reinstalled while still maintaining the connection between the Farm-Bike and the corn sheller.

Additional ergonomic improvements were made later. The shifting cable was shortened and the stem was lengthened to improve shifting accessibility and seating position.

### Last Testing Session: Week 14

During the final week of testing, an interview was conducted with Timothée as part of the project video. The discussion focused on his feedback regarding the Farm-Bike prototype, his impression of the proposed solution for driving agricultural machines, and possible improvements for future iterations.

During this final testing phase, a third pulley system was also installed on a grain sorter. This machine was different from the agricultural machines analysed previously, which allowed the adaptability of the Farm-Bike concept to be further evaluated.

## Future Improvements

A future improvement identified during the final testing phase would be to make the Farm-Bike compatible with flat belt transmissions. Timothée pointed out that many older agricultural machines were originally designed to be driven by flat belts. Adding this compatibility would increase the number of machines that could be powered by the Farm-Bike, especially on farms using older or restored equipment.

This improvement would require an alternative output pulley system designed specifically for flat belts. The pulley geometry, belt tensioning method and alignment system would need to be adapted, since flat belts are more sensitive to lateral misalignment than V-belts. However, this solution would also improve repairability and local reproducibility, as flat belts can be manufactured or repaired from simple flexible materials such as leather or rubber strips cut to the required width and length.

More generally, future development should focus not only on the Farm-Bike itself, but also on compatibility between the bike and a wider range of agricultural machines. A modular output interface with interchangeable pulleys for V-belts and flat belts would make the system more versatile and better adapted to real farm conditions.
